# Section 00 — Orientation: What This Project Is

**Research basis:** project framing (refines the current intro panels 1–4 of
`LearnPage.tsx`). **Slides:** 5. **Completion:** view all 5.

## What this section teaches

Set the table. The reader should leave knowing (a) the site is about
recommendation algorithms — their benefits, their harms, and the possibility of
an _ethical_ alternative; (b) the single distinction the whole course turns on:
**user-_focused_ vs. user-_centered_**; and (c) that this site is both an
explainer _and_ a working demonstration. This section reuses the strongest beats
already written in the current Learn essay, tightened to the 3–4-sentence rule.

---

### Slide 00.1 — Why you're here

- **Icon:** a lightbulb / question mark resolving into an idea.
- **Copy:** Almost everything you watch, buy, or scroll is chosen for you by a
  recommendation algorithm. This site exists to explain how those systems
  actually work, where they quietly cause harm, and — most importantly — how the
  same technology can be rebuilt to genuinely serve you. Think of it as a short,
  honest course, ending in a working example you can try yourself.
- **Source:** project framing.

### Slide 00.2 — They are genuinely useful

- **Icon:** a gift / three stacked cards (shop, play, health).
- **Copy:** Recommendation algorithms are not villains — at their best they are
  remarkably helpful. They surface products you'll actually want, curate music
  and film to your taste, and can even personalize wellness and care. Holding
  that benefit honestly is the only fair starting point for criticizing their
  costs.
- **Source:** project framing (current panel 2).

### Slide 00.3 — The distinction that changes everything

- **Icon:** two arrows — one pointing at a dollar sign, one at a person.
- **Copy:** Most people assume these systems are built _for_ them. But there is a
  crucial difference between being **user-focused** and **user-centered**: many
  algorithms are tuned to their makers' interests — time-on-app, ad revenue —
  while feeling like they serve you. That gap between how a system feels and what
  it optimizes is the subject of this entire course.
- **Source:** project framing (current panel 3).

### Slide 00.4 — Why this is worth your attention

- **Icon:** an hourglass / a clock wrapped around a phone.
- **Copy:** This isn't an abstract concern — these systems mediate hours of nearly
  everyone's day, and the same machinery that recommends a movie can infer your
  mental-health state or quietly narrow what you ever see. Authoritative bodies
  from the U.S. Surgeon General to the American Academy of Pediatrics have issued
  formal advisories about that design. The stakes are real, which is exactly why
  it's worth understanding how the machine actually works.
- **Source:** project framing; foreshadows [[016]] Surgeon General, [[028]] APA,
  [[043]] AAP.

### Slide 00.5 — Explainer _and_ proof

- **Icon:** an open book beside a small running gear / prototype.
- **Copy:** This project does two things at once. It teaches what a
  user-centered algorithm is and how one is built — and then it _shows_ you, with
  a real, transparent preference algorithm you can interact with on the Travel
  page. No algorithm is flawless, but ours is designed from the ground up to put
  you first, and to let you watch it learn.
- **Source:** project framing (current panels 9–11); ties to Section 07.
