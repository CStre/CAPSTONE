# Section 03 — The Human Cost: The Harms

**Research basis:** Synthesis 03 (`/research/synthesis/03-psychological-and-medical-harms.md`).
**Slides:** 9. **Completion:** view all 9.

## What this section teaches

The documented, clinically meaningful harms — **harm by harm, with the real
figures** — produced by engagement-optimized recommendation. Section 04 explains
the _mechanism_; this section establishes _that the harm is real_, drawn from
internal corporate research, clinical reports, causal experiments, and a coroner's
finding. The reader should leave able to name each harm and cite a number for it.

> **This is where honest epistemics matter most.** Do not let it tip into moral
> panic. The harms are real and clinical _and_ concentrated in vulnerable
> subgroups — both are true (slide 03.9 and Section 04 hold that line). The
> credibility of the whole project rests on "whose harm, which feature, what
> mechanism."

### Slide 03.1 — This is medicine, not mood

- **Icon:** a heart with a hairline crack / a medical cross over a phone.
- **Copy:** Start with the register. The clinicians studying this describe
  "clinical-level depression that requires treatment" and "self harm that lands
  people in the ER" — not vague unhappiness. A UK coroner formally ruled that
  algorithmic content "contributed … in a more than minimal way" to 14-year-old
  Molly Russell's death. The stakes here are diagnosable conditions and, in the
  tail, lives.
- **Sources:** [[004]] Facebook Files 2021 (Guarda/Twenge), [[012]] Molly Russell
  inquest 2022.
- **Quote:** "We're looking at clinical-level depression that requires treatment …
  self harm that lands people in the ER" ([[004]]).

### Slide 03.2 — Body image and body dysmorphia

- **Icon:** a mirror showing a distorted reflection / a filtered face.
- **Copy:** Meta's _own_ internal research concluded: "We make body image issues
  worse for one in three teen girls," with 32% of teen girls feeling worse about
  their bodies after Instagram. Clinically, "Snapchat dysmorphia" ties
  filtered-selfie culture to body dysmorphic disorder — a real DSM-5 condition —
  with surgeons reporting patients who want to look like their _filtered_ selves.
  The image feed is, for some, an engine of appearance distress.
- **Sources:** [[004]] Facebook Files, [[009]] Rajanala 2018 (Snapchat
  dysmorphia / BDD).
- **Quote:** "We make body image issues worse for one in three teen girls"
  ([[004]]).

### Slide 03.3 — Depression and anxiety

- **Icon:** a rain cloud over a small figure / a downward mood line.
- **Copy:** Internal Meta slides record that "teens blame Instagram for increases
  in the rate of anxiety and depression." This isn't only correlation: when
  Facebook rolled out across U.S. colleges, it _causally_ worsened students' mental
  health, an effect attributed to "fostering unfavorable social comparisons." And
  moment-to-moment studies find that the more people used Facebook, the more their
  affect and life satisfaction _declined_ over the following hours.
- **Sources:** [[004]] Facebook Files, [[010]] Braghieri 2022 (causal rollout,
  +0.085 SD), [[037]] Kross 2013 (experience sampling).
- **Quote:** the rollout worked by "fostering unfavorable social comparisons"
  ([[010]]).

### Slide 03.4 — Disordered eating

- **Icon:** a plate/scale with a warning / a fork crossed out.
- **Copy:** Clinicians estimate social media plays a role in roughly _half_ of
  their eating-disorder patients. An audit found a major feed pushing
  pro-eating-disorder content to fresh teen accounts within minutes, and both the
  APA and Surgeon General name disordered-eating risk explicitly. The harm is not
  hypothetical — it is in treatment rooms.
- **Sources:** [[004]] Facebook Files (Guarda/Johns Hopkins), [[013]] CCDH 2022,
  [[028]] APA 2023.
- **Quote:** clinicians estimate social media plays a role in "~half" their
  eating-disorder patients ([[004]], Synthesis 03 §1).

### Slide 03.5 — Compulsive, "addictive" use

- **Icon:** a phone with a magnet / a hand that can't put it down.
- **Copy:** Teens told Meta, in its own research, that they feel "addicted" and
  "unable to stop themselves." This isn't a character flaw — it's the designed
  output of variable reinforcement tuned per user (the mechanism Section 04
  unpacks). When people in a controlled study deactivated Facebook, they afterward
  used it _less_ and valued it _less_: usage had exceeded what users themselves
  endorsed.
- **Sources:** [[004]] Facebook Files, [[025]] Bhargava & Velasquez 2021, [[026]]
  Allcott 2020 (deactivation RCT).
- **Quote:** teens feel "addicted" and "unable to stop themselves" ([[004]]).

### Slide 03.6 — Self-harm and suicide

- **Icon:** a fragile thread / a help-line lifeline.
- **Copy:** This is the severe tail, and it is documented. Among teens reporting
  suicidal thoughts, 13% in the UK and 6% in the US traced those thoughts to
  Instagram, per Meta's internal research. Molly Russell, 14, received emails
  titled "10 depression pins you might like" before her death, which a coroner
  tied in part to algorithmic content. Rare, but real — and the reason precaution
  is the right default.
- **Sources:** [[004]] Facebook Files, [[012]] Molly Russell inquest 2022.
- **Quote:** among teens with suicidal thoughts, "13% (UK) / 6% (US) traced them to
  Instagram" ([[004]], Synthesis 03 §1).

### Slide 03.7 — Sleep, eroded at the margin

- **Icon:** a moon with a phone glow / a crossed-out "Z."
- **Copy:** A quieter, near-universal harm: bedtime "doomscrolling" displaces
  sleep. In a medical-society survey, 38% of adults — and 46% of those aged 18–24 —
  said it worsens their sleep, and the aggravator was _emotionally charged content_,
  not just blue light. The engagement objective, which selects for exactly that
  kind of content, keeps you awake by design.
- **Sources:** [[042]] AASM 2025 (doomscrolling sleep survey).
- **Quote:** 38% of adults (46% of 18–24) say bedtime scrolling worsens their
  sleep, driven by "emotionally-charged content" ([[042]]).

### Slide 03.8 — Beyond the feed: dating apps

- **Icon:** a swipe gesture / a profile card being rated.
- **Copy:** Recommendation harm extends past the image feed to any
  appearance-ranked interface. On dating apps, where the _swipe_ is the feedback
  signal, use is associated with self-objectification, body shame, and lower
  self-worth — in both men and women. The same comparison machinery, applied to an
  explicitly appearance-graded surface.
- **Sources:** [[038]] Strubel & Petrie 2017 (Tinder, body image).
- **Quote:** Tinder use is associated with "self-objectification," body shame, and
  lower self-worth in both sexes ([[038]]).

### Slide 03.9 — A corrosive climate — and an honest caveat

- **Icon:** a storm of sharp speech bubbles / a thermometer of "outrage."
- **Copy:** Because the engagement objective rewards what spreads, it _selects for_
  hostility: across 2.7 million posts, each out-group term raised the odds of
  sharing by about 67%, making animosity the single strongest predictor of spread.
  The feed's emotional weather is shaped by arithmetic, not intent. But hold the
  line honestly — across 355,358 teens, generic "screen time" explains _at most
  0.4%_ of well-being, so the harm is **concentrated in the vulnerable tail**, not
  uniform; ask whose harm, which feature, what mechanism.
- **Sources:** [[039]] Rathje 2021 (out-group animosity), [[008]] Orben &
  Przybylski 2019 (the skeptic anchor), [[041]] Common Sense/Hopelab 2024 (agency
  nuance).
- **Quote:** each out-group term raised share-odds by ~67% ([[039]]); generic
  digital use explains "at most 0.4% of the variation in well-being" ([[008]]).

## Section takeaway (the one sentence)

> The harms are **real and clinical** — body dysmorphia, depression, disordered
> eating, compulsion, self-harm, sleep loss, self-objectification, a hostile
> climate — evidenced from internal corporate research to a coroner's finding, yet
> **concentrated in the vulnerable tail**, so the task is to fix the mechanisms and
> protect the vulnerable, not ban the medium.
